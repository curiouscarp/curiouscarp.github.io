It is easy to speculate about the role of derivatives, ETFs, the DTCC, etc. Here, I look through another lens: fundamental analysis. Investopedia defines fundamental analysis as measuring “a security’s intrinsic value by examining related economic and financial factors.”

The SEC requires companies to report their earnings every quarter. At school, I had the chance to use AlphaVantage. The API provides a rich set of macroeconomic data, stock data, and technical indicators. It also includes quarterly earnings reports with data from the balance, income and cashflow sheets. 

Earnings announcements have a significant impact on stock prices. Has the financial health of a company improved? Which metrics improved? Which metrics deteriorated? Companies will also provide forward guidance as an outlook for future performance during earnings announcements. Since investors have expectations as to how a company should perform, the price changes based in relation to the expectations. 

Investment research firms will come up with estimates for EPS and revenue by evaluating financial metrics. Aggregator services then combine the estimates into a “consensus.” Analysts will use the term “beat” or “miss” to describe the performance relative to the consensus. I look at the impact of 10Q data on percent market cap changes from the previous trading day. I am no econometrician, but I am learning so I will give it a shot anyway.

There was a lot of data cleaning involved. For example, AlphaVantage might have had a date for the latest earnings release without the corresponding 10Q data. Other times, the shares outstanding figures were incorrect, or the 10-year yield data was missing for a particular date. I also changed the data building logic from the last iteration to include an earlier close date in case some companies report earnings before the opening bell. The wider range likely contributes to some loss of precision.  

I used XGBoost with k-fold cross validation for hyper parameter tuning with MAE for the loss function. I considered max depth, learning rate, subsample size, and colsample_bytree (240 configurations). I selected K=5 for the cross validation. For splitting the data, I used a GroupShuffleSplit with test size = 1/FOLDS. The code is attached in the references folder. 

After creating the model and I removed features to improve testing losses. Figure 2 shows the importance percentage of various features in the dataset. NetincomeDeltaY, cashFlowDeltaY, totalRevenueDeltaY, and returnOnAssetsDeltaY are y/y percentage changes. The other three values including beta, reportedEPS, and surprisePercentage are raw values. It is possible the beta as calculated from daily returns from the past quarter w.r.t the SP500 is noisy. I also add in a few macroeconomic variables including yearly change in real GDP, and the yearly change in seasonally adjusted core CPI, and the quarterly change in the 10-year. Clearly, Wall Street plays a significant role in determining stock prices.

In the sample set from the five tickers above, I located stocks with similar fundamentals as suggested by Market Chameleon. GME had the highest percentage of overpredictions at 82% on the 38 data points beating all 89 tickers in the validation set. It is plausible that changes in market capitalization before and after earnings releases are artificially suppressed, though there is a loss of precision with the close and open price time range.

For the next analysis, I went through a number of iterations. First I looked at the P/E ratio, then its absolute value, and finally the earnings yield. I realized there are a lot of other variables like stock/bond correlations and accounting for crash risk (e.g. realized volatility) that would need to also be considered. The model gave some extreme numbers during COVID dates. I gained some intellectual humility in the process. I will say that I have learned more about fundamental analysis and some of the factors that contribute to stock price valuation. There are, clearly, a lot of different ways to make money in the markets.
